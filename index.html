PROJECTS

KOJIRŌ IZAKAYA
interactive restaurant menu

The course was Interface Design, and I was working with a talented team of students to create an interactive menu for a restaurant setting. Our vision was to place an interactive mobile tablet in front of every customer in the restaurant, replacing the traditional paper menu.

PROCESS

Our plan was for customers to be able to browse food and drink, and make orders, without having to summon the server. I suggested that we come up with an example restaurant to guide our design.

Because the concept of the "izakaya" (Japanese pub) encouraged long visits with repeated ordering of drinks and small foods, this seemed like the ideal type of restaurant for an on-table ordering menu.

I always start designing on paper. I began by sketching a series of possible layouts, keeping them simple to get through many ideas very quickly. Once I chose a layout that worked, I sketched it out in greater detail.

My concept was to allow the customer to jump to specific food/drink categories with persistent buttons, or allow them to keep swiping through the entire thing as in a traditional menu. A persistent shopping cart would be docked to the right side of the screen, to help people easily understand what they've ordered.

I then took the concept into the digital realm, creating wireframes on the computer.

The goal here was to keep it rough and low detail. We were not worrying about final look yet. This was purely to test the feasibility of the layout.

We took the digital wireframes and placed them into Axure. This tool allowed us to make the wireframes interactive.

We put the interface in front of a few test users, and got feedback. They liked the persistent category buttons and shopping cart. But they were a bit overwhelmed by the fact that you had some things docked to the bottom, and some to the side.

We decided to move the category buttons to the left-hand side of the screen.

It was now time to create a high-detail version of the layout. I created the finished look in Adobe Illustrator, taking care to use fonts and colors which reflected the classy-but-comfortable image of an izakaya.

We had to deliver a pitch video at the end of this process. I brought my final interface design into Adobe AfterEffects (one of my favorite tools) and animated all of the interactions.

A shaded circle represents a user's touch on the interface, and the menu responds with animations between different menu states.

Normally, I'd be at ease shooting live action video for design pitches. But in this case I knew that I could fully count on a fellow teammate. Jay Tseng is an accomplished videographer, and he took a couple of actors to a Japanese restaurant in order to shoot video.

The two ladies acted out interaction with a tablet. When combined with my AfterEffects animation, this helped to visualize the experience of using our interactive menu in the field.
A SUNNY SPOT
short documentary

It was an "intersession" version of the Moving Images course, which meant that this normally full sized course was compressed into a single month and a half. We had to rush to create several short video projects, culminating in a final project to create a short documentary film.

Little did I realize that this time-constrained course would connect me with an adorable older couple who run a Chinese café. I directed the documentary about their story, with the aid of a talented crew of fellow students.

PROCESS

Every documentary begins with a "treatment", a short synopsis of what the film is to be about. In this case, it was created by MacKenzie Ma. She knew of an older couple who ran a Chinese café, famous for their delicious "Xianburgers", and she wanted to document their story.

MacKenzie approached me to join her team. Her and I would work in close collaboration over the next weeks. We were co-directors.

Even documentaries need scripts. We needed to know how the film would work, even before we began shooting. I wrote a quick script that helped the crew to understand what we were going to do.

I proposed that the film have three acts: the introduction of the couple and the cafe, the revelation that they were to retire, and their satisfaction that they were happy and had done a great job.

I would prompt MacKenzie to ask interview questions which teased out this narrative. The film would be spoken in Chinese, so her translation was invaluable, and she really understood the narrative arc that I was proposing.

We actually began shooting on a day the café was closed. This was by design. We needed to shoot "b-roll" footage of the surrounding neighbourhood. This would be intercut with the interview footage, rooting the story in Vancouver's unique Main St. neighborhood.

On the main day of shooting, we set up three cameras at different angles, each manned by a different crew member. I was doing dual duty as director and boom mic operator, holding a microphone over the interview subject.

I reminded MacKenzie of each question to ask, and she did wonderfully. Our café couple delivered entertaining answers to our questions, and delivered the narrative we'd hoped for. After shooting some more interior b-roll footage of the café and its adorable details, we had ourselves a film.

We needed to subtitle the film for our largely English-speaking audience. I had the crew transcribe the couple's responses and translate them into English.

It was okay if it was a too literal translation or the English was rough. I went over their transcription and created my own interpretation in English, checking with MacKenzie to ensure that the meaning was intact. It was okay if some nuances were changed as long as it worked for the intention of the film.

MacKenzie and I edited the film together in Adobe Premiere. I needed her to tell me when certain Chinese sentences began and ended, so the editing would feel right. We chose a music track together and I made sure that the cuts were nicely timed to each section of the song.

I added titles, subtitles, and an ending credits with Adobe AfterEffects. It was also used to perform the "Ken Burns effect" on certain still photographs used in the film.

We screened the film for our teacher and a group of our peers. We could tell from the mood of the room that we had emotionally connected with our audience. We felt proud of our accomplishment, and very happy that we could deliver a retirement gift to the lovely couple who'd been so kind as to star in our film.
ELLA
24 hour app challenge

This wasn't the usual design process. In the Interface Design course, we were given 24 hours to come up with an app design that would help would-be volunteers connect with charitable organizations. It was an opportunity to come up with a finished-looking design in a quick span of time.

PROCESS

I set for myself the constraint that I wanted this app to have a low-bar for entry. I settled on offering social media-based login options from Facebook, Twitter and Google.

This is not a perfect solution for all apps, as it requires people to already be users of one of these social media sites. But it has the advantage of skipping an email-based signup process that can lose people. For this project, I felt it was a fit.

This app needed to be able to suggest charities to volunteers. I wanted to provide a meaningful "feed" of charity suggestions. But to do this, the volunteer needed to state the types of organizations and tasks that they would be interested in.

Taking inspiration from Apple Music, the on-boarding experience for this app would provide a field of "bubbles", describing first organizations and later tasks that the user would be interested in. The user can tap on as many or as few bubbles as they like. This would help to populate the app's feed with appropriate charity suggestions.

The main feed is called "opportunities", and by default shows the app's charity suggestions as determined by the user's choices during the on-boarding experience. These selections will be refined as the user continues to interact with the app. This section also allows you to filter by specific categories.

The user can swipe left and right between different charity options. Each charity is placed on a color-appropriate card which shows imagery of the charity, and the ability to "favorite" it for later, or apply now.

On every charity is a star icon, allowing the user to "favorite" it to a special list.

The star icon is prominantly displayed beside each entry. Clicking on that icon will "un-favorite" the charity, removing it from the list.
PLAYBOY
writer-based website proposal

Interactive Objects & Environments is like the upper-level design boot camp for our school's program. Led by professional design critic Russell Taylor, we were to take on a business client with a "core business problem" and propose a solution for them.

We went to Russell with a list of options: Toys R Us? Blackberry? Playboy...? Yes, that Playboy. Russell liked the last one, and off we went to try and solve the problem of the most infamous magazine in America.

PROCESS

The issue with Playboy was easy to identify: It traded on adult content in an age when adult content was free on the internet, and the magazine's readership was declining. They had just announced that they were removing nudity from the magazine in an effort to pivot to a new identity.

It was the pefect moment to intervene. Could we re-invent them as something new? Could we de-stigmatize the brand and make it cool again?

My team got in a room and teased out the original value of the brand. Largely forgotten by our generation, who knew it only as an adult magazine, Playboy was once respected for its interviews and literary articles. People really did "read it for the articles" at one time. But the medium of a magazine was not the ideal place to connect with today's intelligent audience. How could we revive this?

Our first idea was live broadcasts. We looked at the explosion in popularity of podcasts such as Serial, and we thought this might be a way to connect a modern audience to smart personalities. We thought of a broadcast appropriate to mobile phones, which could either be watched or just listened to.

I was in charge of the user interface and visual design. Working in Adboe Illustrator, I came up with a quick first week design for an app called Playboy Stream.

I combined the famous Playboy bunny logo with a play icon to symbolize this new app. It would allow users to tune into a live show or archived broadcast. If the show was live, the users could influence the direction of the show with an instant vote.

The next week, I took the opportunity to refresh the visual design. The all-black look was on-brand, but it could use a splash of color. We presented it to a panel of critics.

Professor Russell Taylor grilled us on it. He wasn't sold on the idea of a broadcast network as a replacement for the Playboy he knew from his youth. And the content was wrong, he said. Too frivolous. Too many privileged-looked people in the mockup. Fair enough.

We went back to the drawing board...

We ditched the idea of an app. Instead, we'd create a new website that would consider the needs of literary writers, and help connect them with an engaged audience. It would be a combination of magazine and social media blogging platform. Think Medium but with vetted personalities.

It was a website, but we didn't want anything predictable. We wanted to make a splash with the layout. After a little research, I started sketching out new layout concepts. We settled on the concept of a "tree". Each item of content posted by the writer would be a new "leaf", and as they posted, the tree would grow.

I got to work on a finished visual design in Adobe Illustrator. The idea was that the writer had their own dedicated page, showing only the content they posted or re-posted from elsewhere.

The writer would be able to create content by category, and each category posted in a unique color. Depending on the type of post (a quick update, a full article, or a video), the system would make blocks of differing sizes. This automatically grew the "tree" into a unique form as the writer continued to post content.

Our team got to work on a video to pitch the website. We needed to show our website in real-world use. Two of our members, Gurvinder Singh and So Eun Ahn, stood in as a Playboy writer and a Playboy reader, respectively.

I helped to direct the shoot along with Sara Echavarria. Our "writer" posted content on a laptop, and our "reader" read it on a tablet. We'd combine this footage with the website itself, in order to present our vision.

My visual designs were given in slices to other members of the group, in order to animate how users interact with it. Meanwhile, I would create the overall concept for the pitch video.

The elephant in the room is that everyone hears "Playboy" and assumes purile adult content. I decided that we should address this up front and get it out of the way. I thought of the humorous defense always said by Playboy readers: "I read it for the articles". Well, this was not so untrue at one point!

Playing on this phrase, we could remind viewers about Playboy's forgotten literary history, and introduce our writer-focused concept. My video teased the history of the salacious content that Playboy had become known for, before surprising them with Playboy's literary legacy. I animated still shots and added titles with AfterEffects and cut it with the app and actor's footage in Premiere. I set it to a jazz soundtrack evoking the classic mid-century era of Playboy.

The entire website concept was begun and finished in one week. We presented our video in front of a large panel of guest critics.

It was well-received, the video in particular. We collected a lot of feedback about worked and what didn't (a full-screen interface would have been nice while writing a post! - I agree), and our entire team acquired many skills in regard to devising business strategy and user interface design.

This project sparked my passion for creating pitch videos with great visuals, moving text, and a great soundtrack, and it remains one of my particular areas of interest.
ENTERPRISE
Car Drop app proposal

In the middle of Russell Taylor's intensive Interactive Objects & Environments class, my team and I took at look at Enterprise Rent-a-Car. They were known for the "we'll pick you up" concept, where an employee goes to where the customer is when they need a rental car.

It was once a brilliant idea for service. But in this changing landscape of Car-2-Go and Uber, we wondered if they could go further.

PROCESS

Studying the concept of "service design", our team looked at all the places that Enterprise comes into contact with their customers. These contact points are called "touchpoints", and they occur before, during, and even after a customer rents a vehicle. Service design asks us to look at all of these touchpoints, and apply the company values to each one.

We jotted all touchpoints down on sticky notes and arranged them across a timeline, considering both sides of the business/customer relationship.

The touchpoint that stuck in our mind was the "we'll pick you up" concept, where an employee picks up a customer in need of a vehicle. This struck us as the best idea of a few decades ago. Ultimately, the customer still had to get picked up, drive with an employee back to a storefront, and fill out paperwork with a suited employee. Was this really the easiest process in an age of on-demand apps and services?

We had a thought. What if Enterprise could just drop the vehicle off for the customer? My thinking was that self-driving vehicles would be arriving in a few years. Enterprise could position themselves as the vehicle dropoff service early, before the technology was ready. This is similar to how Netflix began as a DVD delivery service in the mid-2000s. They knew streaming would soon be feasible, but it was a way to get there first. Enterprise could tow the vehicle to the customer, or provide alternate transportation for the employee on the return trip. They could even close their storefronts and save money through the idea.

I designed a simple mockup of what the website would look like, populated with vehicles near to the user's location. They could choose a vehicle and it would come right to them.

Professor Russell Taylor and his panel of critics liked they idea, and asked us to focus on a mobile app design.

Our team started to think about the app. Many on the team argued for a traditional grid or list of vehicles. I had a theory that a full-screen visual for each vehicle would make for a more delightful experience, and the customer could swipe left and right through the available vehicles. It was a lot like Tinder.

To illustrate the idea, I very quickly created a mockup in 10 minutes, pictured here. This rather high-fidelity mockup convinced the team that this was the right direction.

I worked on the final design in Adobe Illustrator, and the entire team gave input.

I was never entirely sold on the need to use an entire page to show the user's location (don't they know what city they're in?), but it did provide an opportunity to show beautiful local imagery.

It was my theory to place all "tasks" for the user at the bottom of the screen in grey bars. The green button confirms their choices. The grey bars will rise to the top of the screen if it is still relevant to change the tasks, and they dissappear off the top of the page when the context no longer requires them.

When the grey bar tasks are clicked on, they expand outward as "drawers". Inside each drawer is a larger interface that allows the adjustment for each item.

In this case, the task is to set the date range that a person needs to rent a vehicle. First a calendar is shown, and upon selecting a date, a time selector is shown. Once the user has chosen a day and time, the drawer closes. They can click on the green button to confirm the dates and show the vehicles.

The user is able to swipe between all the vehicles available in their chosen date range.

If they would like to choose a specific type of vehicle, they can click on a button to choose filters for specific vehicle types.

Once the user has chosen their vehicle, they can adjust options such as GPS and insurance.

Once they enter their personal information (which can be saved for another time), they can submit their request for a vehicle, and it will be brought to their location.

After the order has been placed, the user needs to be able to see and review their request.

My theory was to not hide the order in some hidden menu. Instead, a kind of "notification" would be displayed on the "home screen" of the app. Any time you return to the app, the pending vehicle request would be prominantly displayed to them.

We needed to create a video and present it to Russell and his panel of critics. I handed off my interface designs to Linyiang Jiang and Yang Zhang, who animated the app as we had discussed.

Josh McGee and Yang shot video to situate the app in the real world. We were discussing how to portray a person in need of a car. I suggested that the sound of an engine that wouldn't start would go a very long way. I animated the titles for the video, and handed them off to Josh, who finished the editing of the video.

We presented it to the class the following day, where the app design was considered successful.
